Founders ask me some version of the same question all the time: is my engineering good enough? Are we as fast as we should be, as senior, as clean, as good as the teams we compare ourselves to? It is an anxious question and a fair one. It is also the wrong one, because it benchmarks against yesterday's game.

"Good enough versus our peers" is a snapshot of how well you play a game that is ending. The question that predicts where you will be in three years is different, and most founders are not asking it.

## Why the comparison instinct misleads

The comparison instinct fails for a specific reason: every traditional engineering metric assumes humans stay in the loop. Velocity, seniority, code quality relative to peers, they all measure how well your people execute. You can be top-quartile on every one of them and still be structurally stuck, because being an excellent human-in-the-loop engineering org is not the same as being a company a machine can operate. The snapshot can look great while the trajectory is flat, because the snapshot is measuring the wrong axis.

The question that actually predicts the future is about dependence, not quality. How much of your system still requires a specific person to change it, ship it, or even understand it? That number is a direction, not a snapshot, and it is the one that tells you whether the next three years get easier or harder.

## Run the ladder on yourself, honestly

The better self-assessment is to run the ladder honestly. Autocomplete, copilot, supervisor, agentic team, dark factory. Where are you actually, not where does it feel like you are? Most teams that believe they are near the top are two or three rungs lower than they think, because they mistake "we use AI tools a lot" for "our system runs without us." Using a copilot heavily is rung two. Shipping the safe majority of your roadmap with no human writing or reviewing it is rung five. The distance between those is not a tooling gap you can buy your way across. It is legibility and automation you have to build, in order.

Three diagnostic questions cut through the self-flattery fast. Who is the only person who can run your deploy? What documentation would be revealed as a lie if you read it aloud against the running system? And what would actually break if you let an agent open a pull request on the safe eighty percent of your work tomorrow? The answers tell you your real rung, regardless of how you benchmark against peers.

## The reassuring part

Here is the good news buried in the reframe. You do not fix human-dependence by hiring better. Being stuck is not a verdict on your team's talent. You fix it by making the system legible and automating the paths, in sequence, and that is work any competent team can start on Monday. The founders who panic about whether their engineers are good enough are usually asking a question whose honest answer would not even help them. The question that helps, how human-dependent are we, comes with a to-do list attached.

## The line

"Good enough" is a comparison. "How human-dependent are we" is a direction. Only one of them tells you where you'll be in three years.

## So what

Stop grading your team against the field. Start measuring how much of your company would keep moving if the field's single best engineer went on vacation for a month. That number is your real position, and unlike your peer benchmark, you can improve it directly, in sequence, starting now.
