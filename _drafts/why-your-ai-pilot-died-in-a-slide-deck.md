## The thesis

Company runs an AI pilot, sees a modest speedup, can't scale it, quietly shelves it. The postmortem blames the model. The model was fine. The pilot automated typing, which was never the bottleneck, and left the handoffs, gates, and tribal knowledge exactly where they were. A faster keyboard doesn't move throughput, so the pilot had nothing to show, so it died in a deck.

## Beats to develop

- The anatomy of a doomed pilot. Pick a task, give engineers a copilot, measure minutes saved, present the chart, watch it flatten.
- Why it was structurally incapable of succeeding. It targeted the cheap part of the process. (Link the ladder and "You Can't Prove ROI on a Copilot.")
- What a pilot that actually moves the needle looks like. It picks a whole path to production and removes the human from the routine case, gates and all.
- The reframe for the next attempt. Stop piloting tools. Pilot autonomy over one legible slice.

## The line

Your pilot didn't fail. It succeeded at automating the part that was already free.

## So what

If you're about to run another pilot, don't measure how much faster people type. Pick one narrow path and see how much of it can ship without a human. That result is worth presenting.
