The most common mistake companies make with AI is to keep the org chart and bolt a chatbot onto it. Same boxes, same lines, same layers of people doing the same jobs, now with a faster tool. They call it transformation. It is redecoration. The furniture is new and the floor plan is identical.

An AI-native company is not the same company running faster. It is a different arrangement of humans and machines, and the arrangement shows up in the org chart. If yours looks like it did a few years ago with an AI logo in the corner, you have not reorganized. You have just changed the wallpaper.

## The old shape

The traditional org chart is built around human execution. There are layers of people doing the work, handoffs between the layers, and knowledge siloed by role: the people who know the deploy, the people who know the customer, the people who know why the strange part is strange. The whole structure exists to move work and information between humans, because humans were the only things that could do the work or hold the information. Every box either produces something or passes something to the next box.

That shape was correct for a world where people typed every line and remembered every reason. It stops being correct the moment a machine can do the routine execution and a shared system can hold the knowledge.

## The new shape

The AI-native chart redistributes. The middle layer, the executors moving routine work from one stage to the next, thins out, because agents now do the routine execution. The top layer, the judgment, thickens, because that is the work that does not automate: defining what good means, deciding what is worth building, owning whether it turned out well. And crucially, a new piece of infrastructure appears that no old org chart has: a shared world model, a living store of how the company and its systems actually behave, that both the humans and the machines read from. The knowledge that used to be siloed in roles moves into that shared model, where it stops being a person's private asset and becomes the company's.

## What grows and what shrinks, honestly

Some roles grow. The taste-makers who can articulate what good looks like. The metric-owners who own outcomes rather than tasks. The people who write and maintain the living spec, and the people who design the gates that decide what agents may do unsupervised. These are the high-leverage jobs of an AI-native company, and there is more demand for them, not less.

And some roles shrink or transform, and I think it is a disservice to pretend otherwise. The roles whose value was executing the routine middle, or moving information between two other roles, are exactly the ones the machine changes first. The honest version of this future is not "everyone keeps their job and gets a copilot." It is that the work moves up the stack, from doing to defining, and that transition is real for the people living through it. Naming it plainly is how you actually help people make the move, instead of surprising them with it.

## The line

Knowledge lives in a shared world model instead of people's heads. That single change rewrites the org chart more than any tool does.

## So what

Draw your real org chart, the true one, not the official one. Then mark every box that exists mainly to move information between two other boxes. Those are the roles the machine reshapes first, and the shared world model is what replaces the function they were performing. Plan for that shift deliberately, and give the people in those boxes a path up the stack, before it happens to them without a plan.
