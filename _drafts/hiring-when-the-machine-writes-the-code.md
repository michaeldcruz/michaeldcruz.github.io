Most engineering interviews still measure the thing that just got cheap. Write this function. Reverse this list. Implement this algorithm on a whiteboard while someone watches. The whole ritual is built to assess how well a person produces code, and producing code is precisely the capability the machine now has in abundance.

When the routine change gets written by an agent, the ability to write the routine change is no longer an edge. It is table stakes. Keep hiring for it and you are paying a premium for a commodity.

## What the old rubric optimized for

The old rubric made sense in its world. When humans typed everything, throughput was the constraint, so it was rational to select for raw production: who can write the most correct code the fastest. The best engineer was the one whose hands could keep up with their head. That world is ending, and the rubric built for it is now measuring the wrong variable with great precision.

## The signals that actually matter now

The scarce, valuable capacity is judgment, and it shows up as a few concrete, interviewable things.

Can this person define what good means, precisely enough that something other than them could execute it? Can they take their taste, the instinct that tells them this is right and that is not, and externalize it into a spec and a set of checks a machine can run? Can they tell the difference between "the agent said it is done" and "the artifact proves it is done," and do they instinctively reach for the second? And do they own outcomes rather than tasks, measuring themselves by whether the thing worked, not by whether they personally wrote it?

Those are the traits that compound in a world where the machine handles production. They are also, notably, not what a coding screen measures.

## The trap of rehiring your old 10x engineer

There is a specific mistake to avoid, which is reaching for the profile that used to be gold: the engineer who out-produces everyone, holds the whole system in their head, and ships faster alone than a team does together. In the old world that person was your multiplier. In the new one, that same profile can be a bottleneck, because their edge was private throughput and private context, and both of those are now things the system should hold, not a person. The raw producer optimized for exactly the half the machine took over.

## How to interview for it

If you want to hire for judgment, stop asking people to produce and start asking them to define. Less "write this function," more "here is a vague goal, tell me what good looks like and design the gates that would keep an agent honest." Watch how they decide what to escalate and what to automate. Watch whether they reach for evidence or for their own say-so. You are not testing whether they can code. You are testing whether they can direct something that already can.

## The line

The keyboard skill just became the commodity. The judgment behind it just became the whole job.

## So what

Look at your interview loop and count how much of it tests production versus judgment. If it is mostly production, you are optimizing for the half the machine now does, and competing hardest for the trait that just got cheap. Rebalance toward the half that is actually scarce.
