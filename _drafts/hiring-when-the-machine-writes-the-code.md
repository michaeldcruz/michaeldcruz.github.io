## The thesis

Hiring rubrics still mostly measure the thing that just got cheap: the ability to produce code. When the machine writes the routine change, that ability is table stakes, not an edge. The scarce, valuable capacity is judgment. Can this person define what good means, turn their taste into specs and checks a machine can execute, and own an outcome they didn't personally build. Hire for that and you're buying the future. Hire for output and you're buying a commodity.

## Beats to develop

- What the old rubric optimized for, and why it made sense in a world where humans typed everything.
- The new signals. Can they externalize judgment into a spec. Can they distinguish "the agent said done" from "the artifact proves done." Do they own outcomes or just tasks.
- The trap of hiring your old 10x engineer again. Private context and raw throughput are now liabilities, not the edge. (Link "Your 10x Engineer Is Now a Bottleneck.")
- How to interview for it. Less "write this function," more "here's a vague goal, define good and design the gates."

## The line

The keyboard skill just became the commodity. The judgment behind it just became the whole job.

## So what

Look at your interview loop. Count how much of it tests production versus judgment. If it's mostly production, you're hiring for the half the machine now does.
