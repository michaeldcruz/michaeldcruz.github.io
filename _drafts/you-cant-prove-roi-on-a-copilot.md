> Draft. Everyone is trying to attribute business value to their AI code spend and coming up empty. Here's why the measurement keeps failing.

## The thesis

Companies buy a copilot, measure a small speedup, and can't find it on the P&L. They conclude AI ROI is a mirage. It isn't. They automated the fast part, typing, and left every real bottleneck (handoffs, reviews, manual gates, tribal knowledge, the one-person deploy) untouched. You cannot prove step-change ROI from a tool that only shaves the cheapest minute in the process.

## Beats to develop

- The bottleneck was never keystrokes. Restate the ladder (autocomplete to dark factory) and note ROI is roughly flat until you cross from "AI writes" to "no human ships the routine change."
- Why the attribution problem is real but pointed at the wrong layer. You're trying to link token spend to revenue when the value lives in the reliability layer you didn't build.
- ROI is not linear across the ladder. Levels 1 to 3 pay for themselves modestly; the jump comes at 4 to 5 when human time leaves the critical path entirely.
- The honest measurement: not "how much faster did engineers type," but "how much of the roadmap now moves while everyone sleeps, and what did that free the humans to do."

## The line

You can't buy a step change with a tool that optimizes the part that was already free.

## So what

Stop trying to prove ROI on autocomplete. Prove it on autonomy. Measure the work that reaches production without a person, and the number gets legible fast.
