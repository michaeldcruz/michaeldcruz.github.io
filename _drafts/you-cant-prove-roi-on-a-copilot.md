title: "You Can't Prove ROI on a Copilot"
subtitle: "The return on AI shows up when you automate the handoffs, not the typing. Autocomplete ROI is a rounding error. Factory ROI is a step change. That's why the spreadsheets look flat."
---

A company buys a copilot for its engineers, runs it for a couple of quarters, and then someone in finance asks the reasonable question: where is this on the P&L? They go looking for the return, they measure a small speedup on individual tasks, they cannot connect it to anything that moved the business, and they conclude that AI ROI is a mirage. Overhyped. Does not pencil out.

The ROI is not a mirage. They just tried to prove it on the one layer where it barely exists, and then generalized the disappointment to the whole technology.

## The value is roughly flat until "no human ships the routine change"

Think of the ladder from autocomplete to a dark factory. Autocomplete finishes your lines. A copilot takes discrete tasks. A supervisor writes chunks from your specs while you review every diff. An agentic team handles multi-step work. A dark factory ships the routine change with no human writing or reviewing it. The critical fact about that ladder, the one the spreadsheets miss, is that the return is not linear across it.

The low rungs pay for themselves modestly. A copilot really does save an engineer some minutes, and those minutes have some value, and it is a perfectly fine purchase. But it is a rounding error against the cost of the seams: the handoffs, the reviews, the manual gates, the tribal knowledge, the deploy only one person can run. Those seams are where software actually spends its time and money, and the low rungs do not touch them. The return stays roughly flat, small and real, all the way up until you cross the line from "AI writes the code" to "no human ships the routine change." That crossing is where human time finally leaves the critical path, and that is where the step change in ROI lives. Below it, you are optimizing minutes. At it, you are removing the bottleneck.

## The attribution problem is real, but pointed at the wrong layer

The frustration is legitimate. It is genuinely hard to link AI spend to business value. But the reason it is hard is that people are trying to attribute revenue to token spend, when the value does not live in the tokens. It lives in the reliability layer that lets human time leave the critical path, the layer most of these companies never built. You cannot find the ROI of autonomy in the invoice for a copilot, because a copilot is not autonomy. You are auditing the wrong line item and concluding the money is not there, when you simply have not spent it on the thing that produces the return.

## The honest measurement

So stop asking how much faster your engineers type, because that number will always be small and always disappoint. Ask a different question: how much of the roadmap now moves while everyone is asleep, and what did that free the humans to do instead? That is the measurement that captures a step change, because it measures work leaving the critical path entirely, not a task getting incrementally quicker. When you measure autonomy instead of acceleration, the number stops being a rounding error and starts being legible.

## The line

You can't buy a step change with a tool that optimizes the part that was already free.

## So what

Stop trying to prove ROI on autocomplete. You will keep failing, because there is not much there to find. Prove it on autonomy instead. Measure the fraction of the roadmap that reaches production without a person, and what that did to where your people spend their time. On that layer, the return gets legible fast, and you will finally be measuring the thing you were actually hoping to buy.
