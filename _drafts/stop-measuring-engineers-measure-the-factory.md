> Draft. Riffing on the whole industry rush to measure "engineering throughput per person." My take: that metric is measuring the wrong noun.

## The thesis

Everyone is racing to quantify individual engineering output now that AI writes so much of the code. Throughput per engineer, commits per head, "performance doubled." It's the wrong unit. In a shop where a machine writes the routine change, the interesting number is not how productive a person is. It's what fraction of your roadmap reaches production with no human in the loop at all.

Call it autonomy rate. The percent of merged changes that no person wrote and no person reviewed.

## Beats to develop

- Why per-engineer metrics quietly assume the human is still the unit of production. They were designed for a world where a person typed every line.
- The moment you have a factory, the engineer is not the bottleneck and measuring them harder tells you nothing about the constraint.
- What autonomy rate actually rewards: legibility, automated paths, trustworthy gates. You literally cannot raise it without building the boring reliability layer first. (Link to "You Can't Skip to the End.")
- The honest denominator problem: safe eighty percent shipped autonomously is a triumph; one hundred percent, including auth and billing, is a red flag, not a win. The gate is the design.

## The line

You don't get a faster company by measuring people harder. You get one by measuring how little of the work still needs them.

## So what

If you're a leader picking a metric this quarter, pick the one that pushes you toward the factory, not the one that pushes engineers toward the keyboard.
