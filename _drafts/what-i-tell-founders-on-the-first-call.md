Founders come to me wanting the end state. They have seen the demos, they can feel where this is going, and they want it now: autonomous agents shipping features overnight while they sleep. It is the right thing to want. It is just the wrong thing to start with.

So my first call is almost always a redirect, and it is the same redirect every time. I do not ask about their model. I do not ask about their prompts. I ask to see three things, because those three things tell me exactly which rung they are standing on and what the real first project is.

## The three questions

**Show me your deploy.** Not a description of it, the actual thing. How does a change get from an engineer's laptop to a customer? The answer tells me how automated the paths are. If shipping requires a person to remember a sequence of manual steps, there is no factory to put an agent into yet. There is a person with chores, and no agent is going to fix that until the path itself runs without a hand on each button.

**Show me your docs.** The real ones, the ones people actually use, not the ones written for a board deck. The answer tells me how legible the system is. If the true architecture lives in a few people's memory and the written docs are a polite fiction, an agent has nothing to reason from. It will work off the same fiction, without the memory that lets your engineers quietly correct for it.

**Name the one person you can't lose.** Every company has them. The answer tells me where the knowledge is trapped. That person is your most valuable employee and your quietest single point of failure at the same time, and what they know is the first thing that needs to move out of a head and into something shared.

## Why I don't start with the model

Founders expect the conversation to be about the model, because that is what the market talks about. It almost never is. The model is rarely the constraint, and starting there wastes the whole engagement chasing a bigger endpoint when the actual bottleneck is a manual deploy, a fictional doc, and a person who cannot go on vacation. Spend the effort on the environment, not the endpoint, and a modest model does remarkable work. Spend it on the model and you get a smarter agent flailing over the same unreadable system.

## The sequence, in order

From those three answers, the path writes itself, and it is always the same sequence. Make the system legible. Automate the paths. Then, and only then, let agents act. Then reframe the people around the machine that now does the middle. The order is not a preference. It is a dependency chain, and skipping a link is how you get the failure everyone is afraid of: a confident agent doing the wrong thing, fast, over a system nobody can see.

The honest promise I make is not "here is an agent, good luck." It is that we walk the sequence together, in order, so the autonomy they end up with is the kind you can actually trust and leave alone. That is slower than the fantasy and far more durable than it.

## The line

You can't skip to the end. But show me your deploy, your docs, and the person you can't lose, and I can tell you exactly where you start.

## So what

If you are a founder who wants the overnight factory, answer those three questions honestly tonight, before you talk to anyone selling you a model. They will tell you more about your readiness than any demo will, and they cost nothing to ask yourself.
