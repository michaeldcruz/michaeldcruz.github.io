Every company has a few people who are load-bearing not for what they do but for what they know. The engineer who is the only one who understands the deploy. The person who remembers why a system is built the strange way it is built. The one who carries the customer history nobody wrote down. Lose any of them and something important gets harder or stops entirely.

We call these people invaluable, and we mean it as a compliment. It is actually a description of risk. A person whose knowledge exists only in their head is the quietest single point of failure you have, and the most expensive, because the failure mode is not a bug you can patch. It is a resignation letter.

## The liability got worse, not better

You would think more tooling would have fixed this. It made it worse. More systems, more integrations, more moving parts, and each one adds context that has to live somewhere. In most companies that somewhere is a person. The map of how it all fits together got bigger and more valuable exactly as it stayed trapped in a handful of heads. The complexity grew; the place we store our understanding of it did not.

## What a world model is, and isn't

The AI-native move is to externalize that knowledge into a shared world model: a living, queryable representation of how the company and its systems actually behave, that both the humans and the machines read from.

It is important what this is not. It is not a wiki nobody updates, the document graveyard every company already has, accurate the day it was written and drifting ever since. A world model is wired into the work. It reflects how things actually behave because it is connected to the systems it describes and to the agents that act on them. The test is simple: if an agent can read it and act correctly on what it read, it is a world model. If it is a page a human wrote once and no one trusts, it is a wiki.

## The dividend

Move knowledge out of heads and into shared infrastructure and several problems improve at once, from the same act. Onboarding gets faster, because a newcomer can read the model instead of interviewing the veterans. Autonomy gets safer, because an agent has something real to reason from. And resilience goes up, because the person who knew the thing leaving is no longer a crisis. You did not solve three problems. You solved one, the trapped knowledge, and the three were all symptoms of it.

There is a human dividend too, and it is the one people miss. Making yourself replaceable, in this specific sense, is not a threat to your value. It is a promotion. When what you know lives outside your head, you are freed from being the person who has to answer the same question forever, and you get to move up to the work only a person can do: deciding what is worth building and whether it turned out well.

## The line

The most expensive person in your company might be the one you cannot afford to lose. Fix that by moving what they know out of their head.

## So what

Name the three people whose departure would hurt the most, and write down what specifically they know that no one else does. That list is two things at once: the first entries in your world model, and the first risks to retire. Start there.
