You have seen the chart. Output per engineer, climbing since the AI tools landed. It is real. I am not here to argue with the chart. I am here to argue with the conclusion everyone draws from it, which is that the faster individual engineer is the win to chase.

When a person's typing speed stops being the limiting factor, celebrating a faster typist misreads the whole board. The chart is measuring a quantity that is on its way to not mattering, and steering by it points you at the wrong constraint entirely.

## Throughput and private context both flipped

The classic 10x engineer had two superpowers: enormous personal throughput, and a huge amount of context held privately in their head. In the old world, both were pure assets. The person who produced the most and knew the most was your multiplier.

Both of those have quietly flipped into liabilities. Throughput is now cheap, because the machine produces the routine change, so out-typing everyone is out-competing the part that got commoditized. And private context is worse than cheap, it is actively in the way, because an agent cannot read what lives only in a person's head. The system the 10x engineer holds in memory is, to the factory, an illegible system. Their greatest strength, the deep private model of how everything works, is precisely the thing that keeps agents from being able to operate it. The multiplier became the single point of failure the factory has to route around.

## The new high-leverage person externalizes judgment

The person who actually has leverage in an AI-native team looks different. Their skill is not that they produce the most. It is that they externalize their judgment: they take their taste, the instinct for what is good, and turn it into specs, checks, and gates a machine can execute. Their output is not measured in what they personally build. It is measured in how much the system can do correctly without them, because they encoded what they know into the system instead of hoarding it in their head.

That is the real inversion. The old star's value was how much work flowed through them. The new star's value is how much good work flows without them, because they taught the factory to do it.

## The career reframe nobody prepares you for

This is the uncomfortable part, and it is worth saying to the engineers themselves, not just their managers. The shift is from doing the work to defining "good" and owning the outcome. For someone whose identity is built on being the fastest, sharpest producer in the room, that is not an obvious trade to want. It can feel like a demotion, being asked to stop doing the thing you are best at. It is the opposite. It is a promotion to the work that does not commoditize, the judgment and taste and ownership that no model does for you. But it only feels that way if someone names it honestly and helps you make the move, instead of quietly rerouting around you.

## The line

The best engineer on an AI-native team is not the one who writes the most. It's the one the system needs the least and trusts the most.

## So what

Look at your top performers and ask an uncomfortable question: are they your biggest multipliers or your biggest bus-factor risks? If your best people are the ones whose absence would break the most, you have not built a factory. You have built a faster version of the old bottleneck, and the fix is to get what is in their heads into the system, before the org depends on it any harder.
